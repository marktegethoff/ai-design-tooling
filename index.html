The System

The Behavioral Contract

A CLAUDE.md is not a config file. It is a contract that governs how your AI collaborator behaves — encoding your design philosophy, decision hierarchy, and constraints into every session.

What changes when you name it correctly

Called a "config file"

Designers treat it as IT setup. It gets written once, never updated, and becomes stale within a month. Claude's behavior drifts from team standards. No one feels responsible for maintaining it.

compare_arrows

Called a "behavioral contract"

Every edit becomes a design decision with consequence. Designers debate what goes in. Updating it feels like craft, not housekeeping. The contract evolves with the team's standards.

lightbulb

The live demo is required

The phrase only lands if a designer edits the CLAUDE.md and immediately sees AI behavior change in response. Without that visceral before/after, "behavioral contract" is just a slogan. Start every introduction with a live edit and its consequence.

What belongs in a behavioral contract

gavel

Non-negotiables

Brand rules, IP handling, accessibility floor, tone. These are the org layer — they must never be overridden downstream.

design_services

Team standards

Design system tokens, component vocabulary, platform conventions, decision hierarchy. How this team makes decisions.

psychology

Process philosophy

What the AI should never do. What it should always do. How it should surface uncertainty. Anti-patterns that are treated as defects.

warning

Agents and skills are not guardrails

Claude Code agents and workflow skills encode process, not enforcement. They guide the sequence of work and surface the right questions — they do not prevent bad output or enforce compliance. Judgment remains with the designer. The behavioral contract is a statement of intent, not a safety net.

The decision hierarchy

When Claude makes a judgment call, what order should it apply criteria? Encoding this explicitly prevents arbitrary output.

1

Structural correctness

Does the solution solve the actual problem?

2

Conceptual clarity

Is the concept legible without explanation?

3

System coherence

Does it fit the design system and platform conventions?

4

Reduction of parts

Can anything be removed without losing function?

5

Craft precision

Is the execution correct at the detail level?

6

Visual refinement

Does it look right?

tips_and_updates

Add this hierarchy to your team CLAUDE.md verbatim. When Claude produces output that prioritizes visual polish over structural correctness, the hierarchy is missing or not being enforced through prompting.

The System

Agents

Specialist agents are AI personas with defined scope, model, and tools. They are not guardrails — they are structured collaborators that apply a specific lens to a problem.

warning

Agents are not bulletproof

An agent's defined scope is a behavioral instruction, not enforcement. Claude will follow the scope while the conversation is fresh — but in long sessions, or with contradictory prompts, it can drift. Check agent output the same way you check any AI output: read it, verify it, apply judgment.

Two categories of agent

Discipline agents

Single-purpose specialists. Each applies one lens: Historian surfaces precedent. Critic removes unnecessary elements. Architect defines data models. Designer specifies interaction states. They don't make final decisions — they bring specific analysis.

add

Workflow orchestrators

Skills that route work through a sequence of discipline agents. A design workflow might invoke Historian → Strategist → Architect → Critic → Designer in order, each building on the previous. The orchestrator manages the sequence; the designer approves each gate.

Model assignment by role

Assigning the right model to each agent reduces cost and latency without sacrificing quality. The pattern that works:

ModelBest forExamples
HaikuClassification, gates, brief structured tasksProblem gate, design system validation, accessibility checks
SonnetImplementation, synthesis, multi-step reasoningArchitect, Designer, iOS Engineer, QA, research compilation
OpusTaste, quality verdicts, final reviewCreative Director, Distinguished Engineer, facilitated reduction
lightbulb

The model assignment matters most at the quality gate. Running everything through Sonnet is fine for drafting; the final ship/no-ship verdict should go to Opus. The cost difference is negligible per session; the quality difference on taste-level decisions is significant.

The System

Skills

Skills are workflow orchestrators — slash commands that invoke a defined sequence of agents, gates, and pauses. They encode process, not just prompts.

How skills differ from prompts

A prompt

"Design the navigation model for this app." Claude responds once. The quality depends entirely on how well the prompt was written. No gates. No structure. No checkpoints.

compare_arrows

A skill

/studio-design triggers: check prior decisions → Historian pass → Strategist evaluation → Architect constraints → Critic reduction → Designer directions → Accessibility review. Pauses for approval at each gate. The designer controls the sequence.

Skill evolution

Skills evolve as you learn what sequence produces the best output. This is the arc from the EPS.xd OS case study:

1

Simple slash commands

Single-agent invocations with no structure. Fast to write, inconsistent output.

2

Sequenced workflows

Multi-agent sequences with pauses for approval. Consistent structure, but latency from sequential execution.

3

Parallel orchestration

Outer background agents spawn inner specialists simultaneously. One notification per phase instead of one per agent. Dramatically lower perceived latency.

4

Multi-model workflows

Skills that switch models by phase — Haiku for gates, Sonnet for synthesis, Opus for verdicts. Each phase uses the right model for the task.

tips_and_updates

Start with step 1. Build a skill when you repeat the same sequence three times and the sequence matters. Optimize latency only after the sequence is proven.

The System

Memory

Claude has no memory between sessions. Memory files solve this — structured context that loads at session start and accumulates decisions over time.

Two memory tiers

Global memory

Travels with you across all projects. Design foundations. Craft preferences. Process learnings. Things that are true about how you work regardless of what you're working on.

add

Project memory

Scoped to one product or team. Brand principles. System invariants. Decisions made. Things discarded and why. A new session picks up exactly where the last one left off.

What belongs in memory vs. CLAUDE.md

TypeGoes in CLAUDE.mdGoes in Memory
Design principlesYes — behavioral rulesNo
Decisions made this sessionNoYes — persists for next session
Directions discardedNoYes — prevents retreading
Anti-patternsYes — governs behaviorNo
Key file locationsSometimesYes — project-specific state
Named constraintsYes if permanentYes if evolving
warning

Memory files go stale

A memory entry that names a file, function, or decision is a claim about the state of the world when it was written. Before acting on recalled memory, verify it against the current state. Stale memory that contradicts current reality should be updated or removed, not followed.

Pillar 03

Governance

A three-layer inheritance structure so shared context is always present without overriding team autonomy.

lightbulb

Claude Code supports @filename imports inside CLAUDE.md natively. The three-layer stack uses this — no custom tooling required. Each layer imports the layer above it and extends it.

The three layers

1

Org layer — claude-for-eps.md

Capital One non-negotiables. IP handling. Accessibility floor. Brand voice. 3–5 rules maximum. Thin by design — opinionated only on what the org must enforce. Owned by AI Tooling. Changes require review.

2

Team layer — CLAUDE.md

Platform-specific context. Design system vocabulary. Component library rules. Team decision hierarchy. Behavioral contract for this team's AI sessions. Owned by the team lead. Updated at retrospectives.

3

Personal layer — bottom of your CLAUDE.md

In-progress work, personal style preferences, temporary context. Never overwrites org or team layers. Added to the bottom of the team file or in a personal @me.md import.

Team CLAUDE.md — layer import pattern
# @claude-for-eps.md
# (org layer imports automatically — do not edit above this line)

# [TEAM NAME] Design Team
# Platform: [Platform name]

## Team Standards
[Your team-specific content here]

## Design System
[Token names, component vocab, platform conventions]

## Decision Hierarchy
1. Structural correctness
2. Conceptual clarity
3. System coherence
4. Reduction of parts
5. Craft precision
6. Visual refinement

## Anti-patterns (treat as defects)
- [Your team's specific anti-patterns]

## @me.md
# (personal overrides — do not commit)
warning

Keep the org layer thin

If the org layer becomes opinionated about craft — component patterns, visual style, prompt strategy — senior designers will reject it as top-down prescription. The org layer's authority comes from its restraint. It governs only what the org must govern.

Pillar 02

Vocabulary

The words designers use to describe Claude Code determine how they behave with it. Vocabulary is not soft — it governs whether the system compounds or stagnates.

The critical reframes

Old framingNew framingWhy it matters
Training program Infrastructure installation Training implies receiving. Infrastructure implies building something that compounds.
Config file Behavioral contract Config files get ignored. Contracts get debated, updated, and owned.
AI tool AI collaborator Tools are passive. Collaborators have context, preferences, and a working relationship.
Prompt Brief Designers already know how to write briefs. This reuse of existing vocabulary reduces resistance.
Getting Claude to do X Directing the collaborator Agency stays with the designer. The AI executes under direction, not autonomously.
AI-generated output AI-assisted output Authorship remains with the designer. This is not a semantic nicety — it affects IP ownership framing.
lightbulb

Introduce vocabulary before tools

Run the vocabulary workshop before the first Claude Code session. Designers who arrive at the terminal already knowing what a behavioral contract is will configure their CLAUDE.md differently — more deliberately — than designers who encounter the file without that frame.

Pillar 04

Onboarding

New designers should start within the team's behavioral contract — not from a blank CLI. Context arrives first. Tools operate within it.

warning

Self-service terminal install fails for non-technical designers

A "clone the repo and run one command" onboarding only works if every prerequisite is already in place. At Capital One, they won't be. The install must be owned by IT — pre-deployed as part of the standard design tooling package via MDM. Designers should never touch the command line for setup.

What the onboarding package contains

folder

Team CLAUDE.md

Pre-populated with team standards, design system, decision hierarchy, and org layer import. Ready to use on day one.

smart_toy

Discipline agents

The team's agent set installed to ~/.claude/agents/. New members get the same specialists as everyone else from first session.

terminal

Workflow skills

Team-validated workflow skills installed and ready. The right sequence for design, review, and implementation is already encoded.

The first session experience

The designer's first Claude Code session should demonstrate the behavioral contract working — not require them to build it. Recommended first session structure:

1

Show the team CLAUDE.md

Read it together. Explain what each section governs and why it's there.

2

Make one edit, see the consequence

Add a rule. Run a session. Show how Claude's behavior changed. This is the behavioral contract demonstration.

3

Run one real task

A real task from their actual work, not a tutorial exercise. The first session should produce something they can use.

4

Add one personal context entry

What are you working on? What decisions have you made? Their first memory entry, written together.

Pillar 05

The Commit Ceremony

One sentence at the end of every team session. Three months of that and you have institutional memory the AI can use.

Why shared knowledge systems fail

Every team has tried a shared knowledge system. Most die within six weeks. The cause is always the same: contribution is voluntary, asynchronous, and competes with real work. The commit ceremony solves this by making contribution synchronous with work — it's the closing ritual of every session, not a separate task.

Voluntary contribution

"Remember to update the docs after the session." Nobody does. The record is always 3 weeks behind. New members inherit an incomplete picture.

compare_arrows

The commit ceremony

The session isn't closed until someone writes the sentence. One rotation. One sentence. The record grows every session without any separate effort.

What makes a good entry

TypeGood entryWeak entry
Decision "We use a single bottom sheet for all filter states — multiple sheets created context-switching overhead." "We decided on the filter design."
Discarded direction "Rejected tab-based navigation — tabs imply parallel content, our flows are sequential." "We tried tabs and didn't like them."
Named constraint "The transaction list must render within 200ms — anything slower breaks the sense of responsiveness users expect from banking." "Performance matters for the transaction list."
warning

Git is the wrong first surface for non-technical designers

Git write access at an enterprise requires an access request that can take 2–4 weeks. Terminal commits create social pressure to appear competent; designers who don't know how will skip rather than admit it. Use a #design-decisions Slack channel with a one-sentence template as the commit surface. Sync to the shared repo weekly in the background. Designers never touch git to participate in the ceremony.

Slack template — commit ceremony entry
📝 [Team] [Date]
Decision / Direction discarded / Constraint named:
[One sentence. Include the reason — "because X" is the part that makes it useful later.]

Pillar 01

The Capability Proof

Senior designers respond to proof, not process documentation. The adoption hook is not a training deck — it is a real artifact built by a real designer using the real methodology.

lightbulb

The proof must be honest, not polished

A case study that only shows what worked reads as marketing. The proof that moves senior designers shows where the methodology was required (not just helpful), where judgment was irreplaceable, and what the system couldn't solve. Honesty is what makes it credible.

What the proof must demonstrate

person

Designer in control

The AI doing labor the designer directed — not the AI making decisions the designer accepted. Every output visible; every judgment owned.

build

AI doing real labor

Not summaries or text. Architecture decisions, code, design system components, structured analysis — work the designer would have found tedious or inaccessible alone.

verified

Output at the right quality bar

The end product must meet the standard senior designers recognize as legitimate. A rough prototype proves a different (weaker) thing than a production-quality artifact.

The on-ramp is required

Without an immediate next action, the proof converts attention into admiration, not adoption. Every proof asset must link directly to a workshop, a template, or a setup path. The designer finishes reading and has one clear thing to do next.

Case Study

One EPS.xd designer. Full production product. This is the proof.

Your AI Tooling lead built a production-quality digital product solo — full design system, AI features, sync infrastructure, architecture. Using exactly the methodology described in this site. Here's the full arc.

tips_and_updates

The product in this case study is private IP and is not referenced here. What is documented is the methodology — the system structure, the decision-making process, the evolution of skills and agents, and the honest account of where the system required human judgment.

What was built

A production iOS application — full design system, database layer, authentication, AI-powered organization features, sync infrastructure, and a dedicated design canvas for prototyping. Built by one designer. No engineering team. No external contractors. Every architectural decision, every design system token, every implementation was directed through the methodology you're reading about here.

17 tracked sessions. 64 architectural decisions logged. 23 specialist agents deployed. 10 workflow skills built and iterated. The methodology wasn't imported — it was designed and evolved as the product grew. What you see in EPS.xd OS is what actually worked.

The methodology arc

1

First principles, not features

Every system was reduced to its structure before it was built. No feature was added without passing a necessity test: Is this required? What does removing it cost? The CLAUDE.md encoded this discipline from session one.

2

Discipline agents before implementation

No implementation began without a specification. No specification was written without a design review. No design was approved without a Critic pass. The sequence was enforced by workflow skills — not willpower.

3

Memory as institutional record

Every significant decision was committed to the memory system with its rationale. Sessions 15 months apart referenced decisions made in session 2. The AI never re-litigated closed questions because the record was clear.

4

Skills evolved with the work

The first skills were single-agent slash commands. By session 10, they were multi-agent workflows with quality gates. By session 17, they used parallel orchestration with model switching by phase. The methodology grew as confidence in each layer was established.

5

The quality gate held

Every significant artifact — design, implementation, specification — passed through a Creative Director or Distinguished Engineer verdict before it moved forward. SHIP / REVISE / REJECT. Not all work shipped on the first pass. The gate was real.

Where the system required judgment

This is the part most case studies omit. The methodology is not a replacement for judgment — it is a structure that makes judgment more legible.

fork_right

When to skip the workflow

Small tasks don't warrant the full methodology. Knowing when a bug fix is just a bug fix — and when it reveals a structural problem requiring a full design pass — is a judgment call the system cannot make for you.

psychology_alt

When the agent is wrong

Agents produce output shaped by their prompts. A Critic that removes too aggressively, a Designer that proposes the obvious solution — the output must be evaluated, not accepted. The agent is a structured prompt, not an authority.

balance

When principles conflict

The decision hierarchy resolves most conflicts. When it doesn't — when structural correctness and system coherence point in opposite directions — the resolution requires a human who understands both what the product is and what it's becoming.

What this means for your team

The methodology described in this case study is what EPS.xd OS is built from. Not a framework imported from an AI vendor. Not a training program designed for beginners. A system built under real constraints, on real work, iterated across a year of production development.

The five pillars in this site are not theoretical. They are the distillation of what made the difference between a session that compounded and a session that wasted time.

Setup Guide

Getting your team on the system

Sequence matters. Follow this order. Do not start with infrastructure.

For AI Tooling leads

1

Share the capability proof first

Run the case study at a design all-hands or team session before any tooling setup. Senior designers must see what the system produces before they'll engage with how it's structured.

2

Run the vocabulary workshop

One session, 45 minutes. Introduce: behavioral contract, design intelligence infrastructure, brief (not prompt), direct/assisted/authored. End with a live demo of a CLAUDE.md edit changing Claude's behavior.

3

Draft the org layer CLAUDE.md

3–5 rules. IP handling. Accessibility floor. Brand voice non-negotiables. Get legal review. This is the only document that requires that process — keep it thin so it clears quickly.

4

Build a team CLAUDE.md template

One template per platform team. Import the org layer. Add team-specific sections. Include the decision hierarchy. Give teams the template at their first team session, not before — they need the vocabulary to use it well.

5

Work with IT on the onboarding package

Define what needs to be pre-deployed: Claude Code installation, team CLAUDE.md, agent directory, skills directory. Provide a package spec; IT handles deployment via MDM. Target: zero terminal interaction for setup.

6

Launch the commit ceremony

Start with a #design-decisions Slack channel. Give teams the entry template. Run it for 4 weeks before evaluating whether a git-backed memory sync is worth adding. The ritual must exist before the infrastructure matters.

warning

Do not skip steps 1 and 2

Teams that start with infrastructure setup (step 5) without the proof and vocabulary foundation (steps 1–2) treat the CLAUDE.md as a config file, skip the commit ceremony after two weeks, and conclude "it didn't really change how we work." The sequence is causal, not sequential.

For individual designers

1

Read your team CLAUDE.md before your first session

Not skim — read. Understand what each section governs. This is the brief you're working within.

2

Add one personal context entry on day one

What are you working on? What decisions have you already made? What must not change? One paragraph at the bottom of your CLAUDE.md.

3

Run your first real task, not a tutorial

Pick something from your actual work. The first session should produce something you can use. Tutorials don't build the habit — real work does.

4

Contribute to the commit ceremony from session one

Even if your first entry is small. The habit is more important than the quality of early entries.

Templates & Reference

Copy-ready templates, vocabulary reference, and the anti-patterns that indicate the system is breaking down.

Org layer CLAUDE.md — starter template

claude-for-eps.md
# EPS.xd — Org Layer Behavioral Contract
# This file is imported by all team CLAUDE.md files.
# Do not edit without AI Tooling review.

## Non-Negotiables

### IP and Data Handling
- Never include real customer data, PII, or transaction details in prompts
- All AI-assisted outputs are Capital One property; do not reference external products in output
- When in doubt about data sensitivity, exclude it from the session

### Accessibility
- All designs must meet WCAG AA contrast minimum
- Touch targets minimum 44x44pt
- Every interactive element requires a screen reader label

### Brand Voice
- Clear over clever
- Direct over decorative
- Customer-first framing in all copy

## AI Behavior Rules
- AI assists; it does not author or decide
- All AI output requires designer review and approval before use
- Agents and skills are process guides, not guardrails — designer judgment is always required
